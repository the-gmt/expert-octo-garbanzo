1

Do this first

Secure your accounts and change your passwords

‚ñæ

Before anything else, stop the bleeding. If a criminal has access to your email, they can use password reset links to take over every other account you have. Start here.

Change your email password first. Use a strong, unique password you haven't used anywhere else. Enable two-factor authentication if you haven't already.

Change passwords for banking and financial accounts. Prioritize any account connected to money or credit.

Check for accounts you don't recognize. Look in your email's sent folder and review any recent logins in account security settings.

Log out of all active sessions on accounts where you can. Most major services offer this in your security settings.

Your SIN is one of the most valuable pieces of information a criminal can have ‚Äì it can be used to file fraudulent tax returns, apply for government benefits, or open financial accounts. If you believe your SIN has been stolen, contact the Canada Revenue Agency right away.

Contact the Canada Revenue Agency to flag potential fraudulent tax filing activity on your account. You can report by phone or submit a report online.

Over the coming weeks

Dispute every fraudulent account and item

‚ñæ

This is the most time-consuming part of recovery. Each fraudulent item must be disputed individually, in writing, with each creditor and credit bureau. Bureaus typically have 30 days to investigate, but complex cases can take longer.

Write a dispute letter for each fraudulent account ‚Äì include your name, contact information, a description of the item, and why it's fraudulent. Attach your police report number.

Send disputes to each creditor directly as well as to Equifax and TransUnion. Both must be notified separately.

Send everything by registered mail or email with delivery confirmation so you have proof of the date sent.

Keep copies of everything ‚Äì every letter, every response, every case number.

Follow up in writing if you don't receive a response within 30 days.

Know the warning signs

How to tell if your identity has been stolen

Identity theft often goes undetected for months. These are the most common early warning signs.

Unexpected items on your credit report

Accounts, inquiries, or addresses you don't recognize are a strong indicator that someone is using your identity to apply for credit.

Bills or collection calls for accounts you didn't open

Receiving collection calls or statements for accounts you've never opened means a fraudulent account has likely already been opened in your name.

Missing mail or statements

If expected statements stop arriving, a criminal may have changed your mailing address to intercept your mail and hide unauthorized activity.

Denied credit unexpectedly

Being turned down for credit you'd normally qualify for can indicate that fraudulent accounts or delinquencies have damaged your credit profile.

Your CRA account shows unexpected activity

A refund you didn't expect, a return already filed, or changed direct deposit details are signs of tax fraud using your SIN.
